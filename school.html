Financial Trends

Revenue trends are a key indicator of whether a college is bringing in enough money to cover expenses. Examples of revenue include tuition, room and board, government aid, donations and endowment returns.

A college that spends more than it brings in is operating at a loss. Repeated losses can be a warning sign.

Net tuition revenue shows how much money a college brings in per student after institutional discounts and aid.

When net tuition revenue falls, it can put pressure on college finances. But it can also mean a college is giving students more aid and lowering what they pay.

Higher dependence on tuition can make a college more financially vulnerable when enrollment drops.

Enrollment

Drops in enrollment over time can be an early warning sign of financial stress.

Did this institution see a decline in enrollment in 3 of the previous 5 years?

Institutions with higher rates of international students may be more vulnerable to the federal push to make it harder to obtain student visas.

Congress has capped federal loans for new graduate students starting July 1, 2026. This change could hit colleges with higher rates of graduate students who borrow.

Staffing

Drops in staffing can signal that a college is cutting back as a result of financial and enrollment challenges.

Federal And State Aid

Some universities are more dependent than others on state funding and federal grants and contracts. A higher share means the college is more exposed if that funding changes.

*IPEDS shows a negative state-appropriations value in at least one year for this institution. That may reflect a reporting adjustment or reclassification rather than negative aid in the everyday sense.

Universities that heavily focus on research may be more vulnerable to the threat of funding cuts.

Federal Composite Financial Score

The federal government looks at college finances and gives them a score for overall financial health. The scale goes from -1.0 to 3.0, with a score of at least 1.5 indicating the institution is considered financially responsible.

Schools with scores from 1 to less than 1.5 are considered financially responsible, but require additional oversight to keep receiving federal student aid. A school with a score less than 1.0 is considered not financially responsible and must take steps like posting a letter of credit to keep receiving federal aid.

These are the most recent public scores available from Federal Student Aid and cover fiscal years ending between July 1, 2022 and June 30, 2023.

Cash Monitoring

The federal government can delay federal financial aid to colleges with serious accreditation, financial or administrative concerns. It can do so by placing them on what's known as heightened cash monitoring level 2. Historically, most schools on the list are for-profit.
